Sound Basics

Understanding the fundamental properties and characteristics of sound.

What is Sound?

Sound is a form of energy that travels through matter as mechanical waves. These waves are created by vibrating objects and propagate through a medium (typically air) as compressions and rarefactions of molecules.

Key Properties of Sound

Sound has several basic properties that define its characteristics:

Human Perception of Sound

The human auditory system can typically perceive frequencies between 20 Hz and 20,000 Hz, with sensitivity varying across this range. Sound intensity is perceived logarithmically, which is why we use the decibel scale for measurement.

Basic Waveform Types

Audio waveforms come in several basic forms:

Complex Waveforms

Most natural sounds consist of complex waveforms that combine multiple frequencies and harmonics. These can be analyzed using Fourier analysis, which breaks down complex waves into their constituent sine waves.

Harmonic Content

Harmonics are integer multiples of a fundamental frequency that contribute to a sound's timbre. The relative strength of different harmonics gives different instruments their distinctive sounds.

Sound Propagation

Understanding how sound waves travel through different mediums.

Wave Motion

Sound propagates through elastic media as a longitudinal wave, where particles oscillate parallel to the direction of wave travel. The speed of sound varies depending on the medium's properties.

Microphone Placement Techniques

Learn proper microphone placement for optimal audio recording.

Before beginning, ensure you have:

  • Appropriate microphone(s) for the source
  • Microphone stands or mounting equipment
  • Understanding of the source's sound characteristics
  • Acoustic treatment in the recording space (if possible)

Identify where the strongest and most important sound emanates from the instrument or voice.

Consider these general guidelines:

  • Close-miking: 6 inches or less for detailed sound
  • Medium distance: 1-3 feet for balanced sound
  • Room miking: 6+ feet for ambient sound

Point the microphone slightly off-axis (10-20 degrees) to reduce plosives and sibilance.

Effective EQ Usage

Learn how to apply equalization effectively in audio mixing.

Before beginning, ensure you have:

  • A properly calibrated monitoring system
  • Understanding of frequency ranges
  • Reference tracks for comparison
  • Basic knowledge of your EQ tool

Listen for:

  • Muddy or boomy areas (typically 200-400 Hz)
  • Harsh frequencies (typically 2-4 kHz)
  • Excessive sibilance (typically 5-8 kHz)

Cut problem frequencies before boosting desirable ones:

  • Use narrow Q for precise cuts
  • Start with small cuts (2-3 dB)
  • Listen in context with other tracks

Verify that your EQ changes:

  • Maintain proper level balance
  • Don't create masking problems
  • Sound natural and musical
